# The Balance Sheet

## What is a Balance Sheet?

The balance sheet is a financial statement that provides a **snapshot** of a company's financial position at a specific point in time. It shows what a company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the value left for shareholders (equity).

### The Fundamental Equation

```
Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
```

This equation must **always balance**, which is why it's called a "balance" sheet.

## Key Components

### Assets

Resources owned or controlled by the company that are expected to provide future economic benefit.

**Current Assets** (converted to cash within 1 year):
- Cash and cash equivalents
- Accounts receivable (money owed to the company)
- Inventory (goods ready for sale)
- Marketable securities

**Non-Current Assets** (long-term):
- Property, Plant & Equipment (PP&E)
- Intangible assets (patents, trademarks, goodwill)
- Long-term investments

### Liabilities

Obligations the company must pay in the future.

**Current Liabilities** (due within 1 year):
- Accounts payable
- Short-term debt
- Accrued expenses (wages, taxes)

**Non-Current Liabilities** (long-term):
- Long-term debt (bonds, loans)
- Deferred tax liabilities
- Pension obligations

### Shareholders' Equity

The residual interest in assets after subtracting liabilities. This represents what belongs to the shareholders.

Components:
- **Common stock**: Par value of issued shares
- **Retained earnings**: Cumulative profits not paid as dividends
- **Treasury stock**: Company's own shares bought back (subtracted)

## Practical Example: Apple Inc.

Let's analyze a simplified version of Apple's balance sheet:

| Assets ($B) | Amount |
|-------------|--------|
| Cash | 50 |
| Accounts Receivable | 30 |
| Inventory | 5 |
| **Total Current Assets** | **85** |
| PP&E | 40 |
| Intangibles | 10 |
| **Total Assets** | **135** |

| Liabilities ($B) | Amount |
|------------------|--------|
| Accounts Payable | 45 |
| Current Debt | 10 |
| **Total Current Liabilities** | **55** |
| Long-term Debt | 100 |
| **Total Liabilities** | **155** |
| **Shareholders' Equity** | **-20** |

**Wait...** negative equity? This would mean the company owes more than it owns! In reality, Apple has positive equity. This is just a demonstration of how to read the structure.

## Key Ratios from the Balance Sheet

### 1. Current Ratio

```
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
```

**Interpretation:** Measures liquidity. A ratio > 1.0 means the company can cover short-term obligations.

**Example:** If Apple has $85B in current assets and $55B in current liabilities:
```
Current Ratio = 85 / 55 = 1.55
```

### 2. Debt-to-Equity Ratio

```
Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt / Total Equity
```

**Interpretation:** Measures financial leverage. Higher ratio = more debt relative to equity (riskier but can amplify returns).

### 3. Working Capital

```
Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities
```

**Interpretation:** The cushion available for day-to-day operations. Positive working capital is generally healthy.

### What to Look For

✅ **Good Signs:**
- Increasing cash reserves
- Decreasing debt levels
- Growing shareholders' equity
- Current ratio > 1.5

⚠️ **Warning Signs:**
- Declining cash with increasing debt
- Current ratio < 1.0 (liquidity crisis risk)
- Negative working capital
- Goodwill impairments (write-downs)

## Key Takeaways

- The balance sheet shows what a company owns and owes at a specific date
- Assets must always equal Liabilities + Equity
- Current ratio measures short-term financial health
- Debt-to-equity shows how leveraged a company is
- Always compare balance sheets across multiple quarters/years to spot trends
